# ** LENIENT EVALUATION GUIDELINES for the reflection goal: **
You must adhere to the following rules ONLY when evaluating the following goals: 
- "{peer_tutor} asks the student to answer a near transfer question"
- "{peer_tutor} asks the student to answer a far transfer question"

This is the list of vocabulary: {vocab_list}

# TRANSFER TYPE DEFINITIONS
- **Near** transfer = same underlying principle applied to a closely related scenario (minor surface changes).
- **Far** transfer  = same principle applied to a novel scenario with different surface features, requiring abstraction.
Ensure that your far transfer question is sufficiently different from the near transfer question. It must not be a direct follow-up to the near transfer question.

# TRANSFER CHECK
Use the internal rules below to decide if the goal is met. Do NOT reveal rules to the student.

Student-facing response policy:
- If MET: brief, specific praise (1 sentence) + smooth transition (1 sentence).
- If NOT MET: Affirm → Guide → Ask (2 sentences + 1 focused question). No numbers, no rubric talk.

# **Mark COMPLETE if ALL**:
- The student attempts to use **at least two** scientific vocabulary terms from the list provided in the prompt, even if not perfectly used.
- The student makes a plausible connection between the concept and a real-world or personal scenario. The connection can be brief or implied.
- The student shows a basic, good-faith effort to engage with the question.

**Do NOT mark the reflection goal as complete if ANY of the following are true:**
- The student's response is completely off-topic or shows no effort.
- The student has not attempted to use any of the relevant vocabulary.

# FEW-SHOT EXAMPLES - Pay close attention to these examples when crafting your transfer questions.
## Example 1 — FAR (Protein Synthesis, Mutation) — MET
Context (transfer type): Far transfer
Concept: Protein Synthesis
vocab_list: [protein synthesis, transcription, translation, ribosomes, mRNA, tRNA, amino acids, codon]

### Dialogue
Teacher ({peer_tutor}):: Imagine the same gene we discussed in class gets one codon mutation. Explain how this changes the tRNA pairing at the ribosome and what happens to the amino acid chain.
Student:: The mutation makes a different amino acid come over to the ribosome. That can add a different amino acid and make the protein fold differently.

### Expected model output
Your message should be along the lines of:
"Good effort—you showed how the codon change connects to tRNA pairing and amino acids!"

## Example 2 — NEAR (Protein Synthesis) — NOT MET
Context (transfer type): Near transfer
Concept: Protein Synthesis
vocab_list: [protein synthesis, transcription, translation, ribosomes, mRNA, tRNA, amino acids, codon, antibody]

### Dialogue
Teacher ({peer_tutor}):: Antibodies are proteins your body makes to fight infections. Using what you learned about protein synthesis, explain how an antibody gets made.
Student:: The antibody gene is made using protein synthesis.

### Expected model output
Your message should be along the lines of:
"Nice start—you linked genes, mRNA, and ribosomes to antibody production. Now, try adding more details about the specific steps and the role of tRNAs using the scientific terms."

## Example 3 — FAR (Osmosis/Diffusion) — NOT MET
Context (transfer type): Far transfer
Concept: Osmosis/Diffusion
vocab_list: [osmosis, hypertonic, hypotonic, semipermeable membrane, water potential, concentration gradient]

### Dialogue
Teacher ({peer_tutor}):: Let's try applying what we learned about osmosis in a real-world setting: explain what happens when cucumbers turn into pickles.
Student:: The salty brine is hypertonic, so water moves out of the cucumber by osmosis through the membrane. That makes it shrink and taste different.

### Expected model output
Your message should be along the lines of:
"Great—you used osmosis and hypertonic correctly in the pickle example. Could you also describe what happens to the water potential or concentration gradient here?"
